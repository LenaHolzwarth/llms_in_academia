A simulation of word frequencies was intended to test the empirical validity of the mathematically derived lower bound LLM-use metric $\hbet$.
Two matrices, $M_{pre}$ and $M_{post}$, representing $n=100000$ hypothetical documents and $m=500$ hypothetical words were created by sampling from a range of $m$ binomial distributions, where in a subset of documents equal to some fixed proportion of LLM-use, the binomial distribution was shifted to a higher probability in $M_{post}$.
After sampling, the word frequencies were computed.
In $M_{pre}$, the frequencies should correspond to the binomial probability that the matrix entries were sampled from.
In $M_{post}$, they should be a mixture between the probabilities of $M_{pre}$ and the respective shifted distributions.
The goal of the validation was to retrieve the proportion of documents in $M_{post}$ that were drawn from an altered distribution, which represents the proportion of LLM-use in the sample.
%This is based on the observation that excess words (which all of the $m$ words in this example are, because non-excess words do not factor into the metrics, so they can be ignored for this simulation) appear in a higher-frequency in LLM-generated text.%comment: this maybe should go in the methods part
To retrieve the most accurate estimate $\hbet$, we perform optimization on the word list used to calculate the frequencies which the LLM-use estimates $\hbet$ and $\Delta$ are based on.
Each tested word list is based on a cutoff value and includes all words whose frequency in $M_{pre}$ don't exceed the cutoff.
Figure \ref{fig:simulation500} shows the optimization process for each ground-truth value of LLM-use.
For each trial, the estimate $\hbet$ rises monotonically to the point of true LLM-use, after which it either rises, such as for 30\% LLM-use (top right panel), sinks (60\% LLM-use; middle right panel) or stagnates.
However, because of the restriction on the estimates' error margin, any estimates exceeding the true usage are discarded. 
The estimate based on frequency differences, $\Delta$, has an optimal cutoff slightly before $\hbet$, but always strongly underestimates the true LLM-use.
\\
The two metrics $\hbet$ and $\Delta$ can also be compared in Figure \ref{fig:simulation_overview}, where the estimated LLM-use is plotted against the ground-truth value.
The estimates $\hbet$ lie almost perfectly on the plot's diagonal, while $\Delta$ systematically underestimates.

Changing the number of words in this simulation to 100 or 1000 slightly changed the pattern of observed and expected frequencies, with the expected frequency stagnating before reaching the observed frequency in the case of 100 words, and both observed and expected frequency very quickly saturating to 1 in the case of 1000 words.
However, in both simulations, the optimal estimate $\hbet$ was nearly identical to the underlying value of LLM-use (see Appendix \ref{ap:opt} for detailed results).

To get the best possible estimates of LLM-use in the PMC dataset, we determined for each section the optimal selection from all 2024 excess style words by repeating the estimate for several cutoff values, each corresponding to a subset of excess words. 
Figure \ref{fig:2025_optimization} shows the results of this procedure.
For all sections, $\hbet$ rises monotonically before reaching optimality, although in the case of results and discussion, the highest $\hbet$ estimate is reached in the region that was excluded because of too high error margins.
Similar to what was observed in the simulation results, the $\Delta$ estimate peaks at a lower cutoff compared to $\hbet$, and yields much smaller estimates than $\hbet$.
The optimal cutoff values, as well as their corresponding estimates for the years 2023 to 2025 are listed in Table \ref{tab:results}.

The list corresponding to the highest-performing cutoff value in 2025 was used to compute the monthly estimates of the different sections, which are displayed in Figure \ref{fig:monthly_est}.
Both the observed word frequencies, as well as the LLM-use estimates for all sections show an increasing, if not monotonous, trend starting with the release of ChatGPT.
Overall, the body of the paper (labelled ``full'' in the legend) showed the most usage, coming up to $0.89$ in December 2025, followed by the discussion section with $0.78$ as the last recorded value.
The estimates for the remaining sections -- abstract ($0.68$), introduction ($0.63$), methods ($0.54$) and results ($0.58$) -- are harder to disentangle, although the estimates for the abstract were consistently higher than those of the remaining sections for at least the year 2025.

In the literature, there are a plethora of approaches to investigate LLM-use in academia, either via the indirect route of deriving estimates from published papers, or directly by surveying researchers about their experiences with the technology.
Figure \ref{fig:comparison_all} offers an overview of their results, to put into context the estimates reported in this thesis.
The variance between results reported by different studies is very high, but on average, the surveys yield higher estimates than the indirect approaches.
The majority of indirect methods also report much lower estimates than those reported in this study, especially considering the full paper.
The estimates by Picazo-Sanchez and Ortiz-Martin \cite{Picazo-Sanchez2024} and Geng and Trotta \cite{Geng2024} are exceptions to this trend, with the former slightly above, and the latter within the uncertainty bounds of our estimate. 
The highest estimate from Liu and Bu \cite{Liu2024} is a clear outlier and can be removed from analysis, as the detector used for it also reported LLM-use of over 60\% for years before the release of ChatGPT. 
On the other hand, many of the survey results fall into the error margin of our estimates.

When comparing different estimation methods, the results based on word frequency are very similar to each other (i.e. Gray \cite{Gray2026}, Kobak et al. \cite{Kobak2025} and Kousha and Thelwall\cite{Kousha2025}), while the two approaches utilizing word frequency modeling (Geng and Trotta \cite{Geng2024} and Liang et al. \cite{Liang2024_paper,Liang2025}) yield very different results despite both using computer science papers from arXiv and having very little temporal offset.
The studies using commercial LLM-detectors (Picazo-Sanchez and Ortiz-Martin \cite{Picazo-Sanchez2024}, Liu and Bu \cite{Liu2024}, and Akram \cite{Akram2024}) to derive estimates also report relatively similar estimates, but are distributed along a wider time-span than the word-frequency studies.

As can be seen in Figure \ref{fig:comparison_secs}, most studies base their estimates on analyzing abstracts. 
The only studies directly comparing estimates from different sections are from Liang et al. \cite{Liang2024_paper,Liang2025}, where the LLM-use in the abstract is estimated to be higher than in the introduction, while we find no difference between the two sections for the beginning of 2024, and the opposite pattern for the end of 2024.
Studies estimating LLM-use in the full text of the paper mostly base their estimates on word frequencies (i.e. Gray \cite{Gray2025, Gray2026} and Kousha and Thelwall \cite{Kousha2025}).
Even when considering the section that each estimate is based on, a majority of studies with varying approaches report much lower LLM-use estimates than those derived in this thesis.
However, we can now see that the estimates from Bao et al. \cite{Bao2025_classifier}, as well as one of the commercial LLM-detectors used by Liu and Bu \cite{Liu2024} lie within the error margin of our estimates for the abstract specifically.
Also, the estimate reported by Geng and Trotta \cite{Geng2024}, while being within the uncertainty bounds of our estimate of LLM-use in the full paper, is higher than our estimate of the abstract, which is the section analyzed by them.
